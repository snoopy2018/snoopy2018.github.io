Smartphone App

The idea for this app emerged at Laurelwood Elementary School in Santa Clara, California. As a volunteer, I played the card game “SET” with second graders during the breaks from math class. The game trains visual perception and logical thinking. I kept imagining how a smartphone could take over my referee role.
. . .
Technologies used: iOS, Objective-C, C/C++, Xcode, OpenCV, Scilab.
Based on this vision, I developed the smartphone app AI Card Player. The app recognizes the colors and symbols of the playing cards, calculates all permitted combinations according to the rules of the game and presents the solution to the puzzle in a clear and understandable way.

Most of the development has been finding a reliable way to recognize the colors and symbols of the playing cards and translating that model into effective software. Please keep in mind that the visual perception of playing cards is a relatively simple problem for us humans, but it is not a trivial task for a computer system. In contrast, imitating logical reasoning appears to be child's play for a computer system. The implementation of the rules of the game only accounted for a tiny fraction of the development effort. You can find more information about the app at AI Card Player.

Technologies used: iOS, Objective-C, C/C++, Xcode, OpenCV, Scilab.

Analysis of Websites

A customer from Berlin wanted a software that searches the generated websites of an online vehicle market according to criteria and filters out interesting dealers from the flood of offers.
. . .
Technologies used: Python, Beautiful Soup, Selenium WebDriver, JSON, Unix Scripts.
A challenge in this project was the high number of requests to the web server of the online portal (more than 50,000) and the associated time delays or even interruptions by the server.

Our solution was to break the queries down into smaller units and save the intermediate results in a standard format. By this the data can ultimately be merged into the desired list regardless of the Internet connection. At the end, the generated list can simply be imported by a standard program (e.g. Excel or Numbers) and clearly displayed as a table.

This solution made the software system not only more robust and scalable, but also faster.

Technologies used: Python, Beautiful Soup, Selenium WebDriver, JSON, Unix Scripts.

Simulation Model of Distributed Systems

A large automobile manufacturer in the USA was looking for a simulation model for distributed electronic control units (distributed systems). The overriding goal was to develop optimal system architectures for future vehicle models.
. . .
Technologies used: C++, MLDesigner.
As a solution, we have developed a configurable model of distributed systems using a standard simulation tool. The heart of the model is a search algorithm that analyzes all possible communication channels and thus enables the evaluation of selected system architectures. Individually developed evaluation functions also allow a simple analysis of the simulation results.

The simulation model based on statistical methods was finally accepted by the developers and successfully used in the development department of the automobile manufacturer.

Technologies used: C++, MLDesigner.

Software for Safety-Critical Systems

At a well-known Bavarian automotive company, I worked in interdisciplinary engineering teams on the development of safety-critical systems.
. . .
Technologies used: Assembler, C/C++, ASCET, Virtual Component Co-Design (VCC).
As a software engineer, I developed real-time critical software systems that were used with different hardware platforms in different vehicle models. My area of work included the support of the software systems from the first laboratory use through system tests in the vehicle under extreme climatic conditions to support the software systems after production of the vehicles.

Ever more complex systems in the vehicle required the use of new development methods and tools. In a development and research team, I developed and evaluated new development approaches for software in vehicles in order to master the complexity for future vehicle models.

Technologies used: Assembler, C/C++, ASCET, Virtual Component Co-Design (VCC).
