Development of Smartphone App
Development of Smartphone App

The idea for this app emerged at Laurelwood Elementary School in Santa Clara, California. As a volunteer, I played the card game “SET” with second-graders during the breaks in math class. The game trains visual perception and logical thinking. I kept imagining how a smartphone could take over my role as a referee.

Based on this vision, I developed the smartphone app AI Card Player. The app recognizes the colors and symbols of the playing cards, calculates all permitted combinations according to the rules of the game and presents the solution to the puzzle in a clear and understandable way.

Most of the development has been finding a reliable way to recognize the colors and symbols of the playing cards and translating that model into effective software. Please keep in mind that the visual perception of playing cards is a relatively simple problem for us humans, but it is not a trivial task for a computer system. In contrast, imitating logical reasoning appears to be child's play for a computer system. The implementation of the rules of the game only accounted for a tiny fraction of the development effort. You can find more information about the app at the webpage AI Card Player.

Search Program for Webpages
Search Program for Webpages

A customer from Berlin wanted a software that searches the generated webpages of an online vehicle market according to criteria and filters out interesting dealers from the flood of offers.

A challenge in this project was the high number of requests to the web server of the online portal (more than 50,000) and the associated time delays or even interruptions by the web server.

The solution to the problem was to break queries into smaller units and save the intermediate results in a standard format. As a result, the data could ultimately be merged into the desired dealer lists regardless of the Internet connection. The generated list can now simply be imported from a standard program (e.g. Excel or Numbers) and made available to the user as a table.

This solution made the software system not only more robust and scalable, but also faster.

Modeling of Distributed Systems
Modeling of Distributed Systems

General Motors (GM) in the USA was looking for a simulation model for distributed electronic control units (distributed systems). The overriding goal was to develop optimal system architectures for future vehicle models.

As a solution, we developed a configurable model for distributed systems using a standard simulation tool. The heart of the model is a search algorithm that analyzes all possible communication channels and thus enables the evaluation of selected system architectures. Individually developed evaluation functions also allow a simple analysis of the simulation results.

The simulation model based on statistical methods was finally accepted by the developers and successfully used in the development department at GM.

Development of Safety-Critical Systems
Development of Safety-Critical Systems

At the BMW Group in Munich, I worked in interdisciplinary engineering teams on the development of safety-critical systems (e.g. steering systems).

My job as a software engineer was the development of real-time critical software systems that were used with different hardware platforms in different vehicle models. My area of work included the creation of the first prototypes in the laboratory, the subsequent integration into the vehicle with system tests under normal and extreme climatic conditions as well as the support of the software systems after the production of the vehicles.

Ever more complex systems in the vehicle (e.g. Drive-by-Wire) required the use of new development methods and tools. In order to master this complexity for future vehicle models, I developed and evaluated new software development approaches for such systems as part of my doctorate in a research team at the BMW Technology Office in Palo Alto.
