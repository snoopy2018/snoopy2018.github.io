Introducing the Artificial Intelligent Card Player

Practice your rational reasoning and take it to the next level. AI Card Player is a computer vision app that runs on iPhone devices and helps you to learn and master the card game SET. Card Player analyses images from camera or file, identifies color, shape, shading, and number of cards, visualizes game rules, and provides hints for finding sets. The app turns your iPhone into a companion of a fascinating and fun card game.

Previews

The following previews demonstrate main features of the app. The animation of yellow disks represent user's fingertip tapping or dragging elements on screen (for simplicity's sake, taps that close a view are not shown).

Get to know your cards. Tap on a card and the app visualizes its color, shape, shading, and number.

Set or no set? Select three cards and check if they form a set. The app gives you immediately feedback by visualizing what is and what isn’t a set.

Need a hint? Card Player gives you hints whenever you need them. A hint is based on sets in the scene, cards you've selected, and the sets you've already found. It has four detail levels. At level one it shows either color, shape, shading, or number of the three cards that form a set. The level increases after you've tried different card combinations that didn't form a set. At level four, you'll see all four features (color, shape, shading, and number) of the three cards, or in other words, the set is fully disclosed.

Explore all sets. The app finds all possible sets in a group of recognized cards. If there are sets in a scene, tap on the number of sets (top right corner) and get to know them.

Color cast? The app needs to recognize all cards precisely. One obstacle might be a color cast caused by lighting conditions (e.g., blue sky or artificial light). However, the app can adapt to those conditions if you show it a card background.

Tap (longer than 0.5 seconds) with your fingertip on the phone's screen. A rectangle with a sample area in the center will appear. Drag it to any card background. The app will analyze its color and then mark recognized cards with either a red, green, or purple dot.

You may use this feature to measure the color composition of any object in an image. The sample monitor displays red, green, and blue (RGB) colors of the sample area (arithmetic means).

Ready for a challenge? As you gain more experience with the game and our app, you may want to challenge yourself, create and explore other card scenes, or ask questions from a more mathematical point of view. For example, how many sets are possible in a group of N cards? Find a scene of six cards that contains ten sets (is this even possible?). What experiments would you like to do?

Best Practices

The app needs good quality images as input for best results. The following guidelines helped us during development to achieve high card recognition rates, and we hope they will help you to have the best experience with Card Player.

  • Hold your camera parallel to the card surface, otherwise shapes become distorted and difficult to recognize.
  • Keep your camera as steady as possible. Sudden movements blur images that makes it impossible to classify card features.
  • Use proper lighting for your scene. Dark objects are less distinguishable.
  • If you observe an extreme color cast, use the app's color cast filter that adapts to the light source's color.
  • Avoid specular highlights. A game card has a reflective surface, similar to a mirror. If you observe a specular highlight on card images (area without texture), try to move the camera until all cards are outside the spotlight. Or block direct light, if possible, to prevent specular reflection and use only diffuse reflected light, for example, from a wall or ceiling.
  • Please be patient if the app doesn't recognize all cards. Little adjustments, for instance, a slightly different angle between phone and card surface, may already yield to better results.
  • Use the colored markers, which the app draws on each recognized card, as a first indicator whether cards get identified correctly.
  • Always keep in mind that your phone doesn't see objects as you perceive them by your eye-brain system.

Downloads

The video collection below consists of card scenes you can download to your iPhone and start to play with, in case you don't have a deck of cards at hand.

As of this writing (November 2019, iOS 12.4.3), Firefox is surprisingly the only browser that allows to download and save a video on your iPhone. Thus we need to ask you to use Firefox for this procedure:

  1. On your iPhone, navigate in Firefox to any of the videos below.
  2. Tap (longer than 0.5 s) on Download Video button.
  3. Select Download Link.
  4. Select Download Now. By default, Firefox is going to store the video in folder Downloads.
  5. Either tap immediately on Downloads, or navigate to that folder: select Settings > Your Library > Downloads.
  6. Choose video, then select Save Video. Firefox will add a copy to your Photos library.
  7. Done.

Alternatively, if you have access to an Apple desktop or laptop computer, use your favorite browser and click on any of the Download Video buttons below, it will start off the download process; after downloading, open video, for example, with QuickTime Player and share it with your iPhone (in QuickTime Player choose File > Share).

All videos have MPG-4 format and a file sizes of approximately 1 MB (duration about 0.2 seconds).
